I was born in Canada. I grew up here. I built my career here. I am writing this from Markham, Ontario. And I need to say something plainly that most Canadians feel but few are willing to articulate in public: this country is in serious, measurable, structural decline — and almost nothing in the current policy trajectory suggests it will reverse.

This is not a political article. I do not care which party holds power. What I care about is what the data says, because the data is devastating — and the people who live here deserve to see it presented without the qualifiers and euphemisms that Canadian media wraps around every negative economic indicator as if acknowledging decline is somehow unpatriotic. It is not. Refusing to acknowledge it is what is unpatriotic.

---

## The Natural Endowment: What Canada Should Be

Before discussing what has gone wrong, it is worth understanding just how extraordinary the starting position is — because the gap between what Canada has and what Canada does with it is the entire indictment.

Canada is the world's largest producer and exporter of potash, controlling approximately 31% of global output from deposits in Saskatchewan large enough to supply global demand for centuries. It is the second-largest producer of uranium, fuelling clean energy programs worldwide from the Athabasca Basin. It holds the third-largest proven oil reserves on the planet at approximately 170 billion barrels — behind only Venezuela and Saudi Arabia — with the difference being that Canada has the institutional stability, rule of law, and infrastructure to actually extract and export those reserves safely. It is a top-five global producer of gold, a top-three producer of nickel, and a leading producer of diamonds, zinc, copper, and platinum group metals.

Beyond minerals, Canada holds 20% of the world's surface freshwater — in a century where water scarcity is becoming a geopolitical flashpoint. It has 347 million hectares of forest, representing 9% of the world's forested area. It has arable land, fisheries, and a coastline on three oceans. Its geographic position — directly adjacent to the largest consumer economy in human history — is a structural advantage that most countries would build an entire national strategy around.

The institutional foundation is equally strong. Canada has a stable parliamentary democracy, an independent judiciary, a well-regulated banking system that survived the 2008 financial crisis without a single bank failure, and membership in every major international trade and security alliance. It is one of only two AAA-rated G7 nations.

On paper, this country should be one of the wealthiest, most competitive, and most attractive economies on Earth. It has the resources, the geography, the institutions, and the talent pipeline to compete with anyone.

What it does not have is leadership willing to use any of it.

## The Decade of Decline: What the Data Actually Shows

The decline is not a feeling. It is not an anecdote. It is measured, published, and accelerating.

From 2014 to 2024, Canada's real GDP per capita grew by just 3.2%. The OECD average over the same period was 15%. That is not a gap. That is a divergence — a country falling behind its peers at a rate that compounds into a fundamentally different standard of living over a generation. In 2002, Canada's GDP per capita was roughly 80% of the American figure. By 2024, that ratio had fallen to approximately 67%, and the gap has widened every single year since 2015. Statistics Canada's own data shows that relative to the United States, Canada's per capita output in Q3 2025 was 14% below levels observed in early 1997. We are not just falling behind. We are falling behind to a level we have not occupied in nearly three decades.

Real GDP per capita has declined in five of the past six quarters and currently sits near levels last observed in 2017. Investment per worker in business sector industries is approximately 15% lower than in 2006. According to a C.D. Howe Institute study, Canadian workers now receive just 55 cents in business investment for every dollar invested in their American counterparts. For machinery and equipment specifically, the figure is 41 cents on the dollar.

The country is not in a recession in the technical sense — aggregate GDP still grows, barely. But aggregate GDP is a misleading number when you have increased the population by 16.3% in a decade. The Trudeau government increased Canada's population from 36 million to 41.6 million between 2015 and 2025, with temporary residents surging from fewer than one million in 2014 to approximately three million by early 2025. Aggregate GDP went up. GDP per capita — the number that actually measures whether individual Canadians are getting richer or poorer — went sideways and then declined. The headline number masked a per-person stagnation that economists are now calling the defining economic fact of the past decade.

## Capital Flight: A Trillion-Dollar Vote of No Confidence

If GDP per capita is the report card, capital flows are the exit interview.

By the end of 2024, Canadian direct investment abroad outpaced foreign direct investment in Canada by approximately $1 trillion. That number is not a typo. Canada's net FDI stock position is negative one trillion dollars — meaning Canadian individuals, corporations, and pension funds have invested a trillion dollars more in other countries than the rest of the world has invested here. Since 2014, net FDI outflows have consistently outpaced inflows. In 2024, outflows hovered near 4% of GDP while inflows barely exceeded 2.5%.

When Canada's own Maple 8 pension funds — CPP Investments, CDPQ, Ontario Teachers', OMERS, and the rest — deploy capital in the United States, in European infrastructure, in Asian real estate, rather than in Canadian businesses, they are not making a political statement. They are making an investment decision. They are evaluating the risk-adjusted return of deploying capital in Canada versus deploying it elsewhere, and they are choosing elsewhere. Consistently. For a decade.

This is not a market failure. It is a policy failure. Capital does not flee countries with competitive tax rates, efficient regulation, and a clear rule of law. It flees countries where regulatory burden is high, approval timelines are long, tax rates are punitive, and the government signals hostility toward the industries that generate the highest returns on invested capital.

## Brain Drain: Exporting Our Best, Importing Without Strategy

The capital flight has a human equivalent, and it is equally devastating.

In the first quarter of 2025 alone, a record 29,186 Canadians permanently emigrated. The University of Waterloo's elite software engineering program — one of the most competitive in the world — sends the majority of its graduates to Silicon Valley, Seattle, and New York. Canadians applying for U.S. labour certification are disproportionately concentrated in computer science, engineering, and technical management — the exact categories that drive productivity growth, innovation, and high-value job creation. TD Economics describes Canada as a "staging ground" — a country that selects skilled immigrants, integrates them into the labour market, and then loses them to the larger and more lucrative American market.

By 2024, foreign-born Canadian citizens accounted for approximately 60% of Canadian applicants for U.S. labour certification, up from just over half a decade earlier. Canada is importing talent, training it, and then watching it leave — subsidising the American innovation economy with Canadian taxpayer dollars.

Meanwhile, the composition of immigration shifted dramatically. The economic class share of permanent residents fell from 67% in 2010 to 58% in 2024, with the gap filled by a surge in refugee and humanitarian admissions. When temporary workers are included, the economic class represents roughly 32% of total immigration. The country ran what amounts to a real-world experiment: massively increase population through low-skill immigration while simultaneously taxing and regulating the productive economy to the point where high-skill workers and capital leave. The result is visible in every metric that matters.

## The Tax Burden: Paying the Highest Rates for the Lowest Returns

Canadians pay among the highest tax rates in the developed world and receive deteriorating public services in return.

The combined federal-provincial top marginal income tax rate in Ontario exceeds 53%. The capital gains inclusion rate was increased in 2024. Corporate tax rates, while nominally competitive, are embedded in a regulatory environment so burdensome that the effective cost of doing business is substantially higher than the headline rate suggests. The Fraser Institute's annual tax freedom day calculation — the date in the calendar year by which the average Canadian family has earned enough to pay its total tax bill — typically falls in mid-June, meaning Canadians work nearly half the year for the government before keeping a dollar.

The question that every taxpayer should be asking is: what are we getting for this? You work half a year for essentially free. Read that again and let it sink in. Nearly six months of your labour, every single year, goes to the government before you keep a dollar — and in exchange, healthcare wait times are at record levels. Infrastructure is aging. Housing is unaffordable for an entire generation. The military is underfunded to the point of operational incapacity. And yet the government continues to fund international development initiatives, foreign aid commitments, and climate programs abroad while citizens at home struggle with a cost of living crisis that the same government's monetary and fiscal policies created.

## The Housing Bubble and the Debt Trap

Canadian household debt has reached 103% of GDP — the highest in the G7, and the second-highest among all advanced economies after Switzerland. Total household credit market debt reached $3.2 trillion by the end of 2025, or 177% of household disposable income. For every dollar of disposable income, Canadians owe $1.77 in credit market debt. The household debt-to-disposable income ratio now significantly exceeds the level the United States was at before the 2008 housing crash.

The national average home price stood at $663,828 in February 2026. More than half of all Canadian mortgages — 52%, or 3.1 million — will renew by the end of 2027. Of those, 1.3 million are fixed-rate or variable-rate mortgages renewing for the first time since the emergency low-rate period of 2021–2022. These borrowers face material payment increases at a time when real incomes are stagnant — and the pressure is not limited to housing. Canada has among the highest food inflation in the G7, meaning the same households absorbing mortgage payment shocks are simultaneously paying more for groceries, energy, and insurance. The squeeze is coming from every direction at once: the property market is, by virtually any metric, one of the most overvalued in the developed world, the debt levels supporting it are at historical extremes, and the cost of living outside of housing is rising faster than almost anywhere else in the developed world.

## The Investment Climate: Built to Drive Out Entrepreneurs

Everything described above — the productivity collapse, the capital flight, the brain drain, the tax burden, the regulatory friction — creates an investment climate that is structurally hostile to the people who actually create jobs, build companies, and generate the tax revenue that funds public services.

Canada's regulatory approval timelines for major resource projects are measured in decades, not years. The Trans Mountain Pipeline expansion — a project to move Canadian oil to Canadian ports — took over a decade and required the federal government to purchase the pipeline with taxpayer money after the private sector abandoned it. Energy projects, mining approvals, and infrastructure builds are mired in overlapping federal, provincial, and Indigenous consultation requirements that make Canada one of the slowest countries in the developed world to approve and execute capital-intensive projects — despite having more natural resources than almost anyone.

The result is an economy that generates savings but cannot figure out what to do with them at home. One Globe and Mail analysis described Canada as a "sophisticated rentier nation — a country whose expertise is allocating global capital rather than creating domestic value." But even that framing is generous. We do not manage wealth well. Canada holds virtually zero gold reserves — one of the only G7 nations to have sold off its entire gold position. The Canada Pension Plan has posted mediocre returns relative to its global peers since inception and is increasingly deploying capital abroad rather than into Canadian businesses. A trillion dollars in net capital has fled the country in a decade. We do not manage wealth. We export it — and call the export strategy sophistication.

## Where This Goes

Canada is already in a per-capita recession. The stagnation is not new — it has been masked for a decade by aggregate GDP growth driven by population increases rather than productivity improvements. Now that the population growth is slowing (the Carney government has significantly reduced immigration targets), the mask is coming off, and what is underneath is a country that has not meaningfully improved its citizens' standard of living in ten years.

The natural endowment has not changed. The uranium, the potash, the oil, the freshwater — all of it is still in the ground, still untapped, still waiting for a government willing to build the infrastructure and regulatory framework to use it. But the endowment is not the problem, and pointing to it is not a solution. Countries do not decline because they lack resources. They decline because they are poorly governed — and Canada has been poorly governed for a decade by any metric that matters. Productivity has collapsed. Capital has fled. Talent has emigrated. The tax burden has increased while the services it funds have deteriorated. The regulatory environment has become so hostile to investment that the country's own pension funds prefer to deploy Canadian savings in American and European markets rather than at home. None of this is an accident. All of it is the cumulative result of deliberate policy choices made by people who prioritised redistribution over growth, signalling over execution, and short-term political survival over long-term national competitiveness.

If I could buy a put option on Canada, I would. Not because I want the country to fail — I was born here, I was raised here, and the people I love most in the world live here. But because the trajectory is clear, the policy response is inadequate, and the data does not lie. The bet is not that Canada has bad fundamentals. The bet is that the people running it will continue to waste them.
